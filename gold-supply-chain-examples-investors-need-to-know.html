Gold supply chain examples investors need to know

TL;DR:

  • Institutional gold sourcing involves complex supply chains that impact risk, compliance, and liquidity.
  • Global mine-to-market routes, primarily through Swiss refineries, dominate in volume and quality standards.
  • Transparency, traceability, and regulatory compliance are critical factors for risk management and competitive advantage.

For institutional investors, the distance between a gold mine and a trading desk is rarely a straight line. Physical bullion passes through extraction, refining, logistics, and multiple compliance checkpoints before it ever settles in a vault. Paper gold, meanwhile, moves in volumes that dwarf actual physical flows. Understanding these pathways is not a background exercise. It directly shapes pricing power, counterparty exposure, and regulatory standing. This article breaks down real supply chain examples, compares their trade-offs, and gives you a practical framework for evaluating gold sourcing in institutional trading strategies.

Key criteria to evaluate gold supply chains

Before comparing specific supply chain models, you need a working set of criteria. Not all gold is equal from a compliance or operational standpoint, and the wrong sourcing pathway can expose your institution to regulatory penalties, reputational damage, or pricing inefficiencies.

Here are the core evaluation criteria institutional investors should apply:

  • Traceability: Can you document the chain of custody from mine to vault? This is foundational for both internal reporting and external audits.
  • Bar specifications and brand approvals: Only bars meeting gold trading standards qualify for delivery on major exchanges. LBMA Good Delivery and COMEX-approved brands are the benchmarks.
  • Regulatory compliance: AML (anti-money laundering) and KYC (know your customer) protocols must be airtight at every stage. Responsible sourcing certifications, such as those aligned with the OECD Due Diligence Guidance, add another layer of institutional protection.
  • Physical versus paper movement: This distinction is often underappreciated. Key factors in gold trading include recognizing that roughly 20 million ounces clear daily via paper transfers, with minimal actual physical movement between vaults. Conflating these two creates a distorted view of real supply constraints.
  • Secure logistics and storage: Vault location, insurance coverage, and transport protocols all affect settlement risk and cost.
  • Documentation at every step: Incomplete paperwork at any node can delay settlement or trigger compliance flags.

Pro Tip: Prioritize supply chains where documentation is standardized and available on demand. Institutions that can produce chain-of-custody records quickly gain a material advantage during due diligence and regulatory review.

The gap between paper and physical gold is worth pausing on. When paper transfers dominate at 20 million ounces daily, the physical supply chain behind the clearing system becomes a hidden variable. Institutions that understand this dynamic make smarter decisions about physical allocation, basis risk, and delivery obligations.

With evaluation criteria established, let’s examine real-world pathways that illustrate how these factors play out.

Example 1: African mines to Swiss refineries and global markets

The most traveled route in institutional gold sourcing runs from sub-Saharan African mines through Swiss refineries and into the major financial trading centers. Ghana is one of Africa’s largest gold producers, and its output feeds directly into this pipeline.

Here is how the flow typically works:

  1. Extraction: Gold is mined in Ghana, often under internationally monitored environmental and labor standards.
  2. Export: Refined doré bars (partially processed gold) are shipped under secure logistics arrangements to Switzerland.
  3. Refining: Swiss refiners produce LBMA Good Delivery bars meeting strict weight, purity, and hallmarking standards. As 70% of global mined gold is refined in Switzerland, the country functions as the world’s quality control hub for bullion.
  4. Global distribution: Finished bars move to major trading centers. London (LBMA), New York (COMEX), and Shanghai each absorb significant volume based on regional demand and arbitrage conditions.
  5. Vault storage or settlement: Bars enter institutional vaults or are used to back paper positions on clearing platforms.

“Switzerland refines approximately 70% of global mined gold, producing LBMA Good Delivery bars for trading hubs like London (LBMA), New York (COMEX), and Shanghai.”

For institutions, this pathway offers strong market credibility. LBMA-approved bars trade at tighter spreads and face fewer questions during settlement. The compliance burden is real but well-documented, and gold trading regulations governing this route are mature and widely understood.

The risk lies in transit and sourcing opacity. If a mining counterpart cannot provide responsible sourcing documentation, an institution absorbs reputational risk regardless of the refinery’s own standards. Monitoring market insights on regional mining compliance is essential for managing that exposure upstream.

Example 2: Domestic recycling and short-haul supply chains

Beyond large-scale global flows, institutional investors should also consider domestic, recycling-based supply chains. These operate within a single country or region and draw from secondary sources rather than primary mining.

Common sources in domestic recycling chains include:

  • Jeweler scrap: Returned or melted jewelry from retail clients and wholesalers
  • Electronics recycling: Circuit boards, connectors, and components with recoverable gold content
  • Domestic buybacks: Structured programs where institutions or dealers repurchase gold directly from the public or from corporate holders

The flow is simpler. Recovered gold moves to a local refiner, is upgraded to bar or grain specifications, and then enters secure domestic vaulting for settlement. Because the material stays within one regulatory jurisdiction, gold investment terminology around AML and KYC is more straightforward. You are dealing with a known regulatory environment at every step.

Reduced cross-border movement also lowers transit insurance costs and removes customs complexity. Settlement can happen faster, which matters for institutions managing tight liquidity windows.

The trade-off is market access. Domestic recycling chains rarely produce LBMA-approved bars in sufficient volume to compete with primary mine supply at global scale. As physical flows remain small relative to paper clearing volumes, domestic chains can still serve large institutional positions, but they lack the pricing depth and brand recognition of the global route.

Pro Tip: Institutions with significant domestic client bases or regional mandates can improve margin by optimizing short-haul recycling chains. The lower compliance overhead and faster settlement windows often more than compensate for the premium lost on LBMA brand recognition.

The 70% global refining share held by Switzerland reflects why the mine-to-market model dominates institutional volume. But that dominance comes with complexity.

Key considerations when choosing a model:

  • Institutions managing physical delivery obligations on COMEX or LBMA should default to the global mine-to-market pathway.
  • Those managing regional funds or domestic mandates may find the recycling model more efficient.
  • Paper gold clearing suits institutions focused on price exposure without physical settlement requirements.

Building trust in gold trading with counterparties and regulators often comes down to which model you operate in and how well you document it. Choosing the right model for your mandate is not just an operational decision. It is a risk management decision.

Why supply chain clarity matters more than ever for gold investors

Having compared different examples and approaches, here is a candid perspective on what really matters.

Most institutions underestimate how supply chain opacity compounds risk. A compliance gap at the refining stage does not stay there. It travels with the bar through every subsequent transaction, creating latent liability that surfaces at the worst possible moment, usually during a regulatory review or a high-profile counterparty dispute.

Supply chain clarity also improves your negotiating position in ways that rarely get discussed. When you can show a trading counterpart a clean, documented chain of custody, you reduce their credit risk assessment of you. That translates to tighter spreads and faster settlement terms.

The ~20 million ounces clearing daily in paper markets creates an illusion of frictionless liquidity. The physical layer beneath it is far more constrained and far more exposed to sourcing risk. Institutions that treat risk management and transparency as baseline operations, not optional best practices, are the ones positioned to move quickly when physical market dislocations occur.

Proactive supply chain documentation is also a client reporting asset. Wealth management firms that can show end clients where their gold comes from, and that it meets responsible sourcing standards, are building a durable competitive advantage.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common route for institutional gold supply?

The most common route runs from mining operations in countries like Ghana to Swiss refineries, and then to major financial hubs. As Switzerland refines around 70% of global mined gold, it serves as the critical quality checkpoint before bars reach London, New York, or Shanghai.

How much gold clears daily through paper transfers versus physical movement?

Roughly 20 million ounces clear daily through paper transfers, while actual physical movement between vaults remains a small fraction of that volume.

What supply chain features matter most for risk management?

Traceability from mine to vault, regulatory compliance at every node, secure logistics, and full counterparty transparency are the most critical features for institutional risk management.

Can institutions rely solely on domestic gold recycling supply chains?

Domestic recycling chains offer real operational advantages in speed and compliance simplicity, but they typically cannot match the global market access, volume, and LBMA-grade liquidity that international supply chains provide.
