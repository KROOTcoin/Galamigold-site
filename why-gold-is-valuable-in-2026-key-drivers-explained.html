Why gold is valuable in 2026: the demand data

Most investors expect gold to rise when inflation spikes or the dollar weakens. What is harder to explain, and far more instructive, is why gold remains so strong even as those conditions have been mixed. The answer lives in the demand data.

Total gold demand in Q1 2026 rose 2% year over year to 1,231 tonnes, but the real story is the value. That same volume represented a 74% increase in dollar terms, reaching $193 billion. This is what record prices do to value totals. Volume growth was modest. Dollar value was historic.

The surge in bar and coin purchases is particularly revealing. Bar and coin demand hit 474 tonnes, a 42% year-over-year jump. Asian retail investors, particularly in China and India, led this wave. These buyers are not reacting to Fed headlines or short-term volatility. They are making deliberate, long-term decisions to hold physical gold as a store of wealth.

Jewelry tells the opposite story. As prices climbed to record levels, cost-sensitive buyers scaled back purchases. This softness was real, but it did not offset the strength coming from investment demand and official sector buying. Price sensitivity in jewelry is normal. What matters more to understanding gold’s sustained valuation is where the structural demand is coming from.

Pro Tip: When reading gold demand reports, separate volume-based figures from value-based figures. A modest volume gain at record prices represents an enormous shift in market confidence, not just purchasing power inflation.

The real drivers of gold’s value today

Many investors reduce gold’s value to a single variable: real yields. When real yields rise, gold supposedly falls. When they drop, gold rises. That relationship has broken down in 2026, and the reasons why matter enormously for how you approach the metal.

Geopolitical risks remain front and center in supporting gold demand, according to the World Gold Council. This is not vague language. Ongoing tensions across multiple regions, currency instability in emerging markets, and uncertainty around US trade policy have created persistent safe-haven demand that does not correlate with interest rate expectations.

Here is what is actually driving gold’s value in 2026:

  • Central bank accumulation. Central banks purchased a net 244 tonnes in Q1 2026 alone. These are not short-term traders. They are building strategic reserves that reflect reduced confidence in dollar-denominated assets.
  • Retail physical investment. Asian investors piling into bars and coins represent a demand base that is discretionary, conviction-driven, and largely immune to ETF mechanics.
  • Geopolitical risk premiums. Gold carries a built-in premium when trust in financial systems and counterparties erodes. That premium is elevated right now.
  • Portfolio diversification demand. Institutional managers are increasingly treating gold as a drawdown buffer rather than just an inflation hedge.

BlackRock makes this point explicitly, stressing the importance of gold’s diversification role beyond the inflation narrative for portfolio construction decisions. The framing matters because it changes the question investors should be asking. The question is not whether inflation justifies gold. The question is whether your portfolio has adequate drawdown protection.

“Gold’s valuation strength in 2026 is more a shift in demand composition favoring investment and central bank demand, not merely inflation hedging.” — World Gold Council

Understanding which buyers are at the margin is what separates informed gold positioning from reactive speculation. Marginal buyers in gold markets today are central banks and retail physical investors. Both are motivated by trust deficits in conventional financial instruments, not by short-term yield calculations.

Gold as a portfolio tool in volatile markets

Gold’s behavior during equity drawdowns is one of the most underappreciated arguments for holding it. You do not own gold expecting it to grow like a tech stock. You own it because when growth assets sell off hard, gold tends to move differently.

BlackRock’s analysis shows that gold has delivered average returns of 2% during S&P 500 drawdowns exceeding 5% since 2020, outperforming bonds in stress periods. That is not a dramatic return. But it is a positive return when most of your portfolio is bleeding. In portfolio construction terms, that is extremely valuable.

Here is a practical framework for thinking about gold’s role in your portfolio:

  1. Size the position for drawdown protection, not upside. A 5% to 10% allocation provides meaningful ballast without overexposing you to gold’s own volatility during risk-on periods.
  2. Separate your exposure channels. Physical gold, gold-backed ETFs, and gold mining equities behave differently. Know what you own and why.
  3. Do not judge gold by ETF flow headlines. US-listed ETFs saw record outflows of 85 tonnes in March 2026, yet physical demand and central bank buying sustained prices. ETF flows are one data point, not the full picture.
  4. Assess correlation periodically. Gold’s low or negative correlation to equities shifts over time. Revisit your portfolio risk management assumptions at least annually.
  5. Consider liquidity needs. Physical gold provides bearer control and counterparty independence, but ETFs offer easier rebalancing. Match the form to your actual investment horizon.

The investors who struggled with gold in 2026 were largely those who sold during ETF outflow headlines in March, only to watch prices recover as physical and central bank demand absorbed the selling. ETF flows can be volatile in ways that do not reflect the underlying structural demand holding prices up.

Pro Tip: Look at the 12-month rolling correlation between gold and your equity positions quarterly. If it starts rising above 0.3, it may be time to reassess whether your gold allocation is still providing the diversification benefit you expected.

Macro and geopolitical forces shaping gold in 2026

The macroeconomic environment in 2026 has not handed gold an obvious free pass. The Federal Reserve has maintained firm expectations around rates. The US dollar has shown persistent strength in certain periods. These are traditionally headwinds for gold, yet prices hit record highs. Understanding why tells you something important about how the metal works.

A few key forces deserve attention:

  • Dollar strength and rate expectations. Normally, a strong dollar pressures gold prices because the metal becomes more expensive in other currencies. In 2026, sovereign buyers and retail physical investors in emerging markets have offset this dynamic by buying in local currency terms regardless.
  • Geopolitical flashpoints. US-Iran tensions and broader regional instability have contributed to elevated safe-haven flows. Gold’s price consolidation in recent months reflects markets monitoring these developments carefully, not losing conviction in the metal.
  • Supply constraints. Mine production growth has been modest, and recycling has increased but not at a rate sufficient to meaningfully soften prices. Supply is not the story in 2026. Demand composition is.

The interplay between opportunity cost and structural conviction is what makes gold’s 2026 performance so instructive. Elevated rates raise the cost of holding non-yielding assets. Yet central banks and retail investors absorbed that cost deliberately, which signals that their demand is not yield-sensitive. They are buying gold for reasons that yield calculations do not capture.

My take on what most investors are getting wrong about gold

I have spent considerable time studying gold’s behavior across different regimes, and what I keep seeing in 2026 is that investors are asking the wrong question. They are asking whether gold is overvalued relative to real yields. They should be asking whether their portfolios are underexposed to assets that central banks are quietly prioritizing.

The central bank accumulation story is not getting enough attention in mainstream investment discussions. When sovereign institutions add net 244 tonnes in a single quarter, that is not a trade. It is a structural reallocation. These institutions do not buy and sell on quarterly earnings calls or Fed meeting outcomes. They are building reserves for a world where counterparty risk in financial instruments feels more real than it did a decade ago.

Physical gold’s bearer control matters in ways that purely financial instruments cannot replicate. When geopolitical stress turns severe, the ability to hold an asset that carries no counterparty obligation, no issuer risk, and no settlement dependency is not just a hedge. It is a different category of asset entirely.

What I have learned is that gold’s role in a portfolio is most misunderstood by investors who have never experienced a genuine crisis in their home currency or financial system. For investors in Asia and parts of the Middle East, the 42% surge in bar and coin demand is not surprising at all. It reflects an entirely rational response to the world they actually live in, not the world of textbook efficient markets.

My practical advice: treat gold’s diversification benefits as insurance you size correctly, not a speculative position you trade aggressively. The investors who have done well with gold in 2026 held it with conviction through the volatility. Those who tried to time it around ETF headlines missed most of the gain.
