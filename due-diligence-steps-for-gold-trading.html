Due Diligence Steps for Gold Trading: A Compliance Guide

What are the core due diligence steps for gold trading compliance?

Gold trading due diligence is a systematic, risk-based process. Done right, it protects your organization from conflict gold, fraud, regulatory penalties, and supply chain liability. Done poorly, it exposes you to all three at once.

The OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas establishes the five-step framework that regulators worldwide, including U.S. trading entities operating internationally, treat as the baseline standard. The CRAFT Code (Code of Risk-mitigation for ASM engaging in Formal Trade) extends these principles specifically to artisanal and small-scale mining supply chains, which represent a disproportionate share of fraud and compliance risk in global gold markets. Together, these two frameworks define what responsible sourcing looks like in practice.

The five core steps, as recognized across major regulatory regimes, are:

  • Establish a company management system that assigns clear roles, codifies internal policies, and integrates international frameworks into daily operations.
  • Identify and assess risks across the supply chain, including geographic, regulatory, security, and financial risk categories.
  • Design and implement a response strategy with controls calibrated to the severity of identified risks, from enhanced supplier verification to contract-level protections.
  • Carry out independent third-party reviews of your due diligence practices, with audits conducted at least annually under leading regulatory standards.
  • Report periodically to relevant regulatory authorities, with submissions meeting strict content and deadline requirements.

Every section that follows unpacks one of these steps in depth, with practical guidance on documentation, technology, legal compliance, and the specific fraud signals that U.S.-based gold traders encounter most often.

1. How to build a company management system for gold due diligence

A company management system is the operational backbone of any due diligence program. Without it, individual compliance efforts stay fragmented, inconsistently applied, and impossible to audit.

Strong management systems include defined roles, written policies, staff training schedules, and explicit integration of frameworks like the OECD Guidance and CRAFT Code. The key word is “defined.” Vague accountability is the most common reason due diligence programs fail internal audits. Someone specific must own each step, and that ownership must appear in writing.

Building an effective system requires:

  • A designated due diligence officer or team with clear authority to halt transactions pending review.
  • Written internal policies covering supplier onboarding, document verification, risk escalation, and transaction approval thresholds.
  • A supplier register that tracks each counterparty’s license status, audit history, and risk classification.
  • Integration of OECD and CRAFT Code requirements into procurement contracts and onboarding checklists, not just policy documents that sit in a drawer.
  • Regular training cycles for all staff involved in sourcing, procurement, and compliance, updated as regulations evolve.
  • An escalation protocol that specifies what happens when a red flag surfaces, including who decides and within what timeframe.

Continuous professional development strengthens organizational compliance culture in ways that one-time onboarding training cannot. Markets shift, fraud tactics evolve, and new regulatory guidance gets issued. Teams that train once and stop are operating on outdated maps.

Pro Tip: Build your due diligence management system so it can survive staff turnover. If the program lives in one person’s head, it is not a system. Document every procedure, maintain a shared supplier register, and require sign-off trails for every material decision.

2. Identifying and assessing risks in the gold supply chain

Risk identification is where due diligence either earns its keep or becomes a paperwork exercise. The goal is to understand, before a transaction closes, exactly where in the supply chain the exposure sits and how serious it is.

Risk-based due diligence scales the depth and frequency of site visits and document reviews with the severity of identified risks. A supplier operating in a low-risk jurisdiction with a clean audit history warrants a different level of scrutiny than one sourcing from a conflict-affected region with no third-party certification. The framework is proportional, not uniform.

The main risk categories to assess in any gold supply chain include:

  • Geographic risk: Is the gold sourced from a conflict-affected or high-risk area as defined by the OECD? Does the country of origin have weak governance, active armed groups, or documented smuggling routes?
  • Regulatory risk: Does the supplier hold all required licenses and permits? Are they current, or are any pending renewal or revocation?
  • Security and community risk: What are the local security affiliations at the mine site? Are there documented concerns about working conditions, child labor, or community displacement?
  • Financial and fraud risk: Are pricing terms consistent with prevailing market rates? Are payment instructions directing funds to unrelated third parties or offshore accounts?
  • Documentation risk: Are certificates of origin, assay reports, and export permits verifiable through the issuing authority? Do document formats match current official standards?

Global Witness has documented how gold trade data can be used more effectively for due diligence, including cross-referencing export volumes against known production capacity to flag statistical anomalies that suggest laundering or misrepresentation.

Risk scoring in practice

A structured risk matrix helps teams apply consistent judgment across different suppliers and transactions. The table below illustrates a basic scoring approach.

Risk Factor Low (1) Medium (2) High (3)
Country of origin risk Stable, FATF-compliant Moderate governance gaps Conflict-affected or sanctioned
License verification Fully verified, current Minor gaps, pending renewal Unverifiable or expired
Document authenticity All documents confirmed Minor inconsistencies Forgeries detected or suspected
Pricing vs. market rate Within normal range Slight discount Materially below market
Payment routing Direct, named account Third-party intermediary Offshore or unrelated jurisdiction

Scores above 10 should trigger enhanced due diligence, including independent document verification and, where feasible, a site visit or third-party audit before any funds are committed.

3. Designing and implementing strategies that respond to identified risks

Identifying risks without a calibrated response strategy is like diagnosing a problem and then doing nothing about it. The response strategy translates risk scores into specific controls, and those controls must be proportional to what the assessment actually found.

For lower-risk suppliers, standard verification procedures typically suffice: license confirmation, document review, and a signed compliance declaration. For higher-risk counterparties, the response needs to be more intensive. That means enhanced supplier verification, independent assay testing, escrow-based payment structures, and contract terms that explicitly address governing law and dispute resolution.

Key elements of an effective response strategy include:

  • Tiered supplier verification: Apply lighter verification to established, certified suppliers and full enhanced due diligence to new or high-risk counterparties.
  • Document validation against issuing authorities: Never rely solely on seller-provided documents. Cross-reference export licenses, assay certificates, and company registrations against the records of the issuing body.
  • Blockchain and certification schemes: Technology tools can verify chain of custody and detect irregularities more efficiently than manual review alone. Blockchain-based provenance tracking and schemes like the London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) Responsible Gold Guidance provide verifiable audit trails.
  • Escrow and conditional payment structures: For cross-border transactions, require funds to be held in a licensed bank escrow account and released only after positive assay results and export clearance are confirmed.
  • Supplier contracts with compliance clauses: Contracts should require suppliers to maintain their licenses, submit to audits, and notify you immediately of any regulatory action against them.
  • Escalation protocols for critical findings: Define in advance what happens when a document is flagged as potentially forged or a supplier fails a verification check. The decision tree should not be improvised mid-transaction.

Pro Tip: When evaluating a new gold supplier, request that assay testing be conducted by an independent, accredited laboratory of your choosing, not one the seller recommends. Seller-nominated labs are a documented vector for falsified results.

4. Monitoring, auditing, and reporting for responsible gold sourcing

Designing a due diligence system is one thing. Keeping it honest over time is another. Continuous monitoring, independent audits, and transparent reporting are what separate a functioning compliance program from one that looks good on paper until something goes wrong.

Independent third-party audits are mandated at least annually under key regulatory standards. The independence criterion matters: an auditor with a commercial relationship to the supplier being reviewed cannot provide the objectivity the process requires. Third-party reviewers should have no financial interest in the outcome and should be selected by the trading entity, not the supplier.

Effective monitoring and reporting practices include:

  • Key performance indicators (KPIs) for due diligence: Track metrics like the percentage of suppliers with current verified licenses, the number of transactions flagged for enhanced review, and the average time to resolve a compliance exception.
  • Internal audit cycles: Conduct quarterly internal reviews of supplier files, documentation completeness, and transaction records, separate from the annual third-party audit.
  • Incident logging: Maintain a formal log of every red flag identified, the action taken, and the outcome. This log is your primary evidence of a functioning compliance program if a regulator asks.
  • Regulatory reporting deadlines: Under standards aligned with OECD requirements, reports must be submitted within 90 days of completing the annual review cycle. Missing that window is itself a compliance failure.
  • Public transparency commitments: Where your organization has made public commitments to responsible sourcing, reporting should align with those commitments and be consistent across internal and external disclosures.
  • Governance integration: Due diligence reporting should feed into board-level governance, not sit in a compliance silo. Senior leadership needs visibility into material risks and audit findings.

Key insight: Periodic reporting is not just a regulatory obligation. It creates an internal discipline that forces teams to confront gaps they might otherwise defer. Organizations that treat reporting as a compliance burden tend to discover problems later and at greater cost than those that treat it as a management tool.

When non-compliance or suspicious activity surfaces during monitoring, the response must be documented, escalated, and resolved before the next transaction with that counterparty proceeds. Continuing to transact while a compliance issue is under review is a pattern regulators treat as willful blindness.

5. Ensuring legal and regulatory compliance for U.S. gold traders

The U.S. legal environment for gold trading sits at the intersection of several overlapping frameworks. Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Countering the Financing of Terrorism (CFT) regulations apply broadly to precious metals dealers under the Bank Secrecy Act. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) requires dealers to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) when transactions exhibit red flags, and Customer Due Diligence (CDD) rules mandate identity verification for counterparties above defined thresholds.

AML/KYC regulatory compliance carries real financial consequences for U.S. traders who fall short. Penalties for AML violations in the precious metals sector have grown substantially in recent enforcement cycles, and regulators have shown willingness to pursue individual liability, not just corporate fines.

Critical legal compliance requirements for U.S.-based gold traders include:

  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD): Verify the identity of all counterparties, including beneficial owners of legal entities, before executing transactions above applicable thresholds.
  • Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs): File with FinCEN within 30 days of detecting a transaction that meets SAR criteria, including structuring, unusual payment routing, or counterparties on sanctions lists.
  • OFAC sanctions screening: Screen all counterparties against the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list before any transaction. A single missed hit can result in civil penalties regardless of intent.
  • Contract governing law and dispute resolution: Gold sale agreements must explicitly define the governing law and dispute resolution mechanism. Without these clauses, enforcing a contract in a foreign jurisdiction becomes prohibitively difficult and sometimes impossible.
  • Documentation retention: U.S. AML regulations require dealers to retain transaction records for a minimum of five years. This includes KYC documents, transaction records, and any SAR filings.
  • Import and export compliance: Cross-border gold transactions require compliance with U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) reporting requirements and, where applicable, export control regulations.

Pro Tip: Never treat an advance fee request as a negotiating point. Any counterparty demanding payment of a “compliance fee,” “export clearance fee,” or “government processing fee” before gold changes hands is running an advance fee fraud. Legitimate gold exporters do not require buyers to pay compliance or clearance fees upfront. Disengage immediately and report the contact to the relevant financial crime authority.

Legal advisors with specific experience in precious metals transactions are worth the cost. A contract drafted by a generalist attorney who has never seen a gold sale agreement is a liability, not a protection. Clauses around inspection rights, assay dispute resolution, and force majeure in cross-border shipments require sector-specific knowledge.

6. Verifying gold supply legitimacy: documentation and traceability

Document verification is the most concrete, testable part of any gold trading due diligence checklist. It is also where fraud most often surfaces, because forged documents are the primary tool of advance fee schemes and conflict gold laundering operations.

Essential gold trading documents for any transaction include the dealer’s license, assay report from an accredited laboratory, certificate of origin, sales contract, export permit, and tax compliance certificate. Each document must be verified against the records of its issuing authority, not simply accepted at face value.

What to verify and how

Dealer licenses and permits: Confirm the license number directly with the issuing government ministry or regulatory body. Do not rely on a copy provided by the seller. License numbers that do not appear in the official registry, or whose format is inconsistent with current numbering conventions, are a strong indicator of forgery.

Assay reports: Request that assay testing be conducted by an independent, accredited laboratory. Cross-reference the certificate number with the laboratory’s own records. Fraudulent assay certificates frequently use the letterheads of legitimate labs while citing certificate numbers that do not exist in those labs’ systems.

Certificates of origin: These must be issued by the relevant government authority in the country of production, not by the seller or an intermediary. For gold sourced from artisanal mining regions, chain-of-custody documentation tracing the gold from mine site to point of export is required under OECD-aligned standards.

Chain of custody documentation: Every handoff in the supply chain, from mine to refinery to storage to export, should be documented with signed records. Gaps in the chain of custody are a compliance failure even when the gold itself is legitimate.

Payment routing verification: Confirm that payment instructions direct funds to a bank account in the name of the selling entity, in a jurisdiction consistent with the transaction. Payment directed to a private account in an unrelated third country is a documented fraud mechanism, not an administrative quirk.

For traders evaluating gold trading counterparties, a structured verification checklist applied consistently across all new suppliers is the most reliable way to catch document irregularities before they become financial losses.

7. Tools and technologies that improve supply chain transparency

Technology has changed what is possible in gold supply chain verification, though it has not eliminated the need for human judgment. The most effective programs combine digital tools with experienced analysts who know what to look for.

Blockchain-based provenance tracking allows each transfer of gold to be recorded on an immutable ledger, creating a verifiable chain of custody from mine to market. Several industry initiatives have piloted this approach for artisanal mining supply chains, where documentation gaps are most common. The limitation is adoption: blockchain provenance only works when every participant in the chain is enrolled, and coverage remains uneven across producing regions.

Certification schemes provide a different kind of assurance. The LBMA Responsible Gold Guidance, the Fairmined Standard, and the Fairtrade Gold Standard each set requirements for sourcing, labor conditions, and environmental practice that certified suppliers must meet and maintain. Buying from certified suppliers does not eliminate due diligence obligations, but it does provide a credible baseline that reduces the depth of verification required for routine transactions.

Trade data analysis tools, as documented by Global Witness, allow traders to cross-reference declared export volumes against known production statistics and customs records. Discrepancies between what a supplier claims to export and what official trade data shows can indicate laundering, smuggling, or simple misrepresentation. This kind of data-driven screening is particularly useful for identifying suppliers whose stated capacity does not match their documented history.

For large-scale acquisitions or M&A involving gold mining assets, technical due diligence goes beyond legal compliance to include mine plan reviews, geotechnical data assessment, and operational viability analysis. These assessments identify supply chain risks that document review alone cannot surface, including geological constraints that affect production forecasts and operational factors that affect delivery reliability.

Digital gold instruments, including tokenized gold products, offer built-in transparency features that physical gold supply chains often lack. Understanding how these instruments handle custody and verification provides useful reference points for what best-practice documentation looks like.

8. How to handle non-compliance findings and suspicious activity

Finding a problem during due diligence is not a failure. Failing to act on it is. The response to a non-compliance finding or suspicious activity report determines whether your organization is protected or exposed.

The first principle is documentation. Every finding, however preliminary, must be logged with the date, the nature of the concern, the evidence reviewed, and the action taken. This log is your primary defense if a regulator later questions why a transaction proceeded or was halted.

When a document is flagged as potentially forged or a counterparty fails a verification check, the correct sequence is:

  1. Suspend the transaction pending resolution. Do not proceed while a material compliance issue is open.
  2. Escalate internally to the designated due diligence officer or compliance committee, per your written escalation protocol.
  3. Seek independent verification of the disputed document or counterparty claim through the issuing authority or a qualified third-party verification service.
  4. Make a documented decision: proceed, proceed with conditions, or disengage. The decision must be signed off by an authorized person and retained in the file.
  5. File regulatory reports where required: If the finding meets SAR criteria under FinCEN rules, file within the required 30-day window. Do not delay filing while waiting for a transaction outcome.
  6. Disengage cleanly when warranted: If the finding indicates fraud, cease all communication with the counterparty and any associated brokers. Retain all documents and communications for potential law enforcement referral.

The U.S. State Department’s Africa Gold Advisory documents the most common fraud patterns in detail, including advance fee schemes, forged document packages, and broker chain manipulation. Recognizing these patterns early is the difference between a compliance finding and a financial loss.

Risk management frameworks that include pre-defined non-compliance response procedures are significantly easier to execute under pressure than ones that require improvised judgment at the moment a problem surfaces.

9. Internal training and capacity building for due diligence teams

A due diligence program is only as good as the people running it. Training is not a one-time event at onboarding; it is an ongoing function that keeps your team current with evolving fraud tactics, regulatory changes, and best practices.

Continuous professional development is a recognized component of strong compliance culture, not an optional add-on. Regulators increasingly look at training records as evidence of organizational commitment to compliance, particularly when evaluating whether a violation was willful or inadvertent.

Effective capacity building for due diligence teams includes:

  • Annual regulatory updates: Dedicate at least one training session per year specifically to changes in AML/CFT rules, OFAC sanctions updates, and any new OECD or CRAFT Code guidance.
  • Fraud pattern recognition training: Use real case studies, including documented advance fee fraud examples, to build pattern recognition skills. Abstract policy training does not prepare staff for the specific tactics they will encounter.
  • Document verification workshops: Train staff to identify the specific security features, format conventions, and numbering systems used by the issuing authorities in your key supply chain countries. A team that knows what a genuine Guinean export license looks like is far harder to deceive than one that does not.
  • Scenario-based exercises: Run tabletop exercises that simulate a high-pressure transaction with embedded red flags. These exercises reveal gaps in escalation protocols and decision-making under time pressure.
  • Cross-functional training: Procurement, legal, finance, and compliance teams all touch due diligence at different points. Training that crosses departmental lines reduces the risk of a red flag being seen by one team and not communicated to another.

Staff who understand why due diligence matters, not just what the checklist says, make better judgment calls in ambiguous situations. The goal is a team that can recognize a novel fraud pattern even when it does not match a documented template.

10. Periodic review and continuous improvement of your due diligence system

A due diligence system that was adequate two years ago may not be adequate today. Supply chain risks evolve, fraud tactics adapt, and regulatory requirements change. Periodic review of the system itself, separate from transaction-level monitoring, is what keeps the program current.

Annual system reviews should assess whether the risk categories in your assessment framework still reflect actual market conditions, whether your supplier verification procedures have kept pace with changes in issuing authority formats and systems, and whether your escalation protocols have been tested and found to work as designed. If the annual third-party audit identifies gaps, those findings should feed directly into a documented improvement plan with assigned owners and deadlines.

Continuous improvement also means learning from near-misses. When a red flag is caught and a transaction is halted, the post-mortem should ask how the risk got as far as it did and what earlier in the process could have caught it. These lessons are more valuable than any policy document because they are grounded in your actual supply chain, not a generic framework.

Building transparent partnerships within gold supply chains is itself a continuous improvement mechanism. Suppliers who understand your due diligence requirements and have invested in meeting them are lower-risk counterparties over time. The relationship itself becomes a compliance asset.

For traders working across multiple supply chain jurisdictions, a centralized review calendar that tracks audit cycles, reporting deadlines, license renewal dates, and training schedules prevents the kind of administrative gaps that regulators find most concerning. Compliance failures are rarely dramatic; they are usually the result of something routine that nobody was tracking.

In practice, this means Galamigold applies tiered verification to all counterparties, with enhanced scrutiny for suppliers operating in higher-risk jurisdictions or without established audit histories. Supplier onboarding requires full documentation review, including license verification against issuing authority records, independent assay confirmation, and chain-of-custody documentation for every shipment. The broader lesson from Galamigold’s experience is that due diligence and commercial efficiency are not in tension. Counterparties who have been through a rigorous onboarding process are faster and easier to transact with on subsequent deals.

Pro Tip: In evolving markets, the biggest due diligence risk is complacency with established suppliers. A counterparty who passed verification two years ago may have changed ownership, lost a license, or been flagged by a regulator since then. Build annual re-verification of all active suppliers into your compliance calendar, not just onboarding checks for new ones.
